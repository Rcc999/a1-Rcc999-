# Ex.5.1 – Biped Reflection

The MiniPi looks unnatural because the kinematic controller assumes the base perfectly follows a pre-planned trajectory without accounting for balance — a quadruped can stay statically stable during single support since three legs still form a support polygon, but a biped's center of mass immediately falls outside the support area when one foot lifts, so the robot just tilts and wobbles throughout the whole cycle. On top of that, the IK only controls the end-effector position and not its orientation, so the ankle pitch joint ends up in a toe-down configuration rather than keeping the foot flat on the ground, which makes the walking look even more unnatural. To fix this, we would need at minimum a balance controller (e.g., based on the Zero Moment Point or capture-point methods) that actively adjusts the base trajectory in real time, plus orientation targets in the IK to enforce flat foot contact. If we deployed this motion directly on real hardware, the robot would fall immediately — the controller assumes the base can track any trajectory regardless of the physical forces required, which is simply not true for a biped that needs to actively generate ground reaction forces to stay upright. In short, the kinematic walking controller works well enough for statically stable robots like quadrupeds or hexapods, but it completely ignores the dynamic balance problem that makes bipedal locomotion fundamentally harder.
